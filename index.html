Authentic Materials in Action

Four classroom activities — two for speaking, two for writing — built on real TikTok, TED, BBC, National Geographic and Medium content, and delivered through TikTok, Padlet and two web apps I coded myself.

01 — Introduction

What this ebook is about

This ebook documents four activities I designed for a B2 English class, each one built around a piece of authentic material — content made by real speakers for real audiences, not written for a textbook. Nothing here was simplified, graded or invented for the classroom.

Each activity is presented the same way: a screen from the material, the link to the original source, a short explanation of what students actually do, and the reason that particular material earns its place in a speaking or writing lesson. The theoretical foundation follows in section 04, and every source is listed at the end.

Why these four

A TED Talk clip circulating on TikTok, a TEDx talk on gamification, two international news reports, and a personal blog post paired with a BBC video. Together they cover the range a B2 student meets outside class: informal spoken English, academic argument, scientific journalism, and personal opinion writing.

02 — Context Description

Who these activities are for

StudentsYoung adults, approximately 17–22 years old, in higher education.
LevelB2 (Upper-Intermediate) on the CEFR. They can follow extended speech, but hesitate when they have to defend an opinion under time pressure.
ProfileDigital natives. Social media, learning platforms, video games and language apps are part of their daily environment — the material meets them where they already are.
NeedsFluency and confidence in unscripted speaking; lexical range and a clear position in academic writing.
SettingBlended: face-to-face discussion plus asynchronous digital work (Padlet walls, browser-based workspaces).
Session length35–60 minutes per activity, extendable across two sessions.

03 — Selected Authentic Material

The four activities

Two speaking activities open the sequence; two writing activities close it. The amber rule marks speaking, the teal rule marks writing.

Speaking · Activity 1 TikTok B2

Try Something New for 30 Days

Matt Cutts' TED Talk, met where students actually meet it — on a TikTok feed.

Source tiktok.com/@monicahidalgo839/video/7663259472340454663

What students do

They watch the clip, then talk: about a habit they would try for thirty days, what would get in the way, and what actually changed the last time they committed to something small. The talk is the prompt, not the lesson — the goal is unscripted speech about their own goals and routines.

Why this material

Cutts speaks at natural speed with real hesitation and rhythm, so students hear English as it is spoken rather than as it is recorded for a coursebook. The 30-day-challenge premise gives every student something to say without special knowledge, and the vocabulary it supplies — habit, challenge, stuck in a rut, motivation, personal growth — is exactly the lexical field the discussion needs. Delivering it through TikTok matters: the material arrives in the same format and the same feed students already scroll, which removes the distance between "English class" and their real digital lives.

Speaking · Activity 2 Padlet TEDx

The Gamification Dilemma in Modern Classrooms

An asynchronous video debate: Technology Innovators against Traditional Educators.

Wall padlet.com/yvcriollo/the-gamification-dilemma-in-modern-classrooms
Video youtu.be/tgg59icEFIE
Video youtu.be/mOssYTimQwM

Objective

By analysing a real talk on gamification and taking part in an asynchronous debate, students strengthen critical thinking, fluency and argumentation — using academic vocabulary from education and technology, stating a position, and responding to an opposing one.

What students do

They watch a TEDx talk on gamification (Gabe Zichermann's is the suggested example), then the class splits in two. Technology Innovators argue that game mechanics raise engagement, motivation and retention. Traditional Educators argue that constant rewards erode intrinsic motivation and lower students' tolerance for frustration. Each student records a two-minute argument video on Padlet defending their assigned role, then watches an opponent's video and posts a one-minute rebuttal that questions a specific point, politely and with evidence.

Why this material

A TEDx talk exposes students to real accents, colloquial phrasing and an argument built to persuade an audience — not to illustrate a grammar point. The topic is one they live daily, so both sides are genuinely arguable, and the assigned role forces them to defend a position they may not hold, which is where argumentation gets learned. The two-minute cap on Padlet equalises speaking time: every student produces the same amount of language, and the asynchronous format lets quieter students rehearse without the pressure of a live floor.

Language it produces

Target lexis: intrinsic motivation, rewards, engagement, retention. Target structures for B2: formal recommendation ("Schools should set screen-time limits to prevent cognitive fatigue") and advanced conditionals ("If teachers were to eliminate games entirely, students could lose their connection to essential digital tools").

Writing · Activity 1 Web app I built BBC · NatGeo

Editorial Inquiry & Content Analysis Workshop

Two real environmental reports, six academic terms, and a 200–250 word critical synthesis.

Live app bless-your-heartt.onrender.com

What students do

The workshop runs in four steps. They read at least one of the two authentic reports (audio available for each). They study six curated academic terms pulled straight from the articles. They take a True/False challenge on the precise scientific claims. Finally they write two critical questions of their own — one on scientific method, one on socio-economic feasibility — and a 200–250 word analytical synthesis.

The academic lexicon

Rewilding
Large-scale ecological restoration that returns native species so nature can regulate itself again.
Trophic cascade
The chain reaction through a food web when a keystone species or apex predator is added or removed.
Anthropocene
The current geological epoch, defined by human activity as the dominant force on climate and environment.
Microplastics
Synthetic fragments under 5 mm from industrial waste and degraded consumer products.
Ecological resilience
An ecosystem's capacity to absorb and recover from severe disturbance without losing its structure.
Mitigation strategies
Technical, legislative and social measures designed to reduce or eliminate an environmental hazard.

The comprehension check

StatementAnswer
Passive conservation — simply fencing off habitat — is enough to restore trophic cascades.False — active rewilding with keystone species is required.
Scientists confirmed synthetic microplastics 36,000 feet below sea level.True
Reintroducing predators causes riverbank erosion by driving herbivores to the water.False — predators disperse herbivores, letting riverside vegetation regrow and stopping erosion.
Deep-sea microfibres come from industrial runoff and floating synthetic textiles.True

Two model syntheses

Option 1 — the ecological case (BBC, supporting the article). The questions: how does reintroducing keystone species trigger a trophic cascade, and what empirical evidence shows it accelerates carbon capture? And how can governments balance that urgency against farmers' fears for their livestock? The 226-word answer explains the biology of the cascade, argues that self-regulating forests are the strongest natural shield against warming, and resolves the human conflict with protected wildlife corridors and state compensation schemes.

Option 2 — the policy case (National Geographic, criticising the article's solution). The questions: why do synthetic polymers bioaccumulate so heavily in benthic organisms at 36,000 feet? And can municipal circular-economy incentives and biotechnology outpace slow international treaties? The 228-word answer accepts the science on the Mariana Trench but rejects treaty-dependence — decades slow, unenforced, ignored by industry — and proposes municipal Extended Producer Responsibility laws, circular-economy incentives, and subsidised biotech (plastic-eating engineered enzymes, algae-based bioplastics) that stop toxic plastic at the factory.

Why this material

BBC News and National Geographic are written for informed adult readers, so the syntax and lexical density are genuinely B2–C1 — no simplification to unlearn later. Both reports make falsifiable claims, which is what makes a True/False check meaningful and a critical question possible: students can only challenge a text that actually asserts something. And because the two reports pull in different directions — restoration that works, pollution that persists — the writing task has a real argument to take a side in rather than a summary to reproduce.

Writing · Activity 2 Web app I built Medium · BBC

Writing a Blog Response

One blog post, one BBC video, one question: is social media good for us? Take a position.

Live app fiiddnocchpionad.onrender.com

What students do

They read Noemi Di Stefano's Medium essay "Is Social Media Killing Us?" — a personal argument that platforms amplify envy and comparison — and watch a BBC My World reporter answer the same question on camera, arguing the opposite: social media has saved lives and built genuine connection. Then they write a 200–250 word response that takes a clear position. Agreeing or disagreeing is required; summarising is not enough.

How the app supports the writing

Everything lives on one page, with nothing uploaded anywhere. A Listen button reads the blog quote aloud through the browser's speech synthesis. A quick check of three True/False questions on the video gives feedback explaining each answer. The workspace holds two analysis fields — the post's main argument, one example she uses — the agree/disagree buttons, and the textarea. Two live meters run underneath: a word count against the 200–250 target, and lexical diversity, calculated as unique words divided by total words. Three of the four steps tick themselves off — step 2 when the quiz is answered, step 3 when the word count enters range, step 4 when a position is chosen — and finishing all four releases the final button and a burst of confetti.

Why this material

The pairing is the point. One source is written and personal, the other spoken and journalistic, and they disagree — so the student cannot write a safe summary. They have to weigh two real voices and land somewhere. Neither text was simplified: the Medium post keeps its informal digressions, the BBC video its unscripted delivery. And the topic is one every student already has a private opinion about, which is what turns a writing exercise into something they want to say.

The evidence behind the lexical diversity meter

The meter is not decoration. Chan, Lowie and de Bot (2016) compared the same learners' written and spoken production and found writing yields significantly higher lexical diversity — planning time lets students reach for words speech does not give them time to retrieve. Showing the ratio live makes that affordance visible, and nudges students to use it.

04 — Theoretical Foundation

Why these activities support learning

Five ideas hold this sequence together. Each one is doing real work in the four activities above.

Authentic Materials

Authentic materials are texts produced by real speakers for real audiences, with no pedagogical intent (Nunan, 1989). Their value is exposure to language as it is actually used — the hesitations in Cutts' talk, the lexical density of a BBC report, the digressions in a personal blog. Gilmore (2007) argues their strongest contribution is motivational: students recognise the content as real, and the task stops feeling like an exercise. Every material here is unedited, and every one is linked to its original source in section 06.

Writing and Speaking Process

Both skills are treated as processes, not products. The writing activities run through planning (identify the main argument, note an example), drafting (the 200–250 word workspace), and revising against visible criteria — word count, lexical diversity, position taken. The speaking activities do the same: students prepare an argument, record it, listen to an opponent, and produce a considered rebuttal. Chan, Lowie and de Bot (2016) supply the reason the two skills are worth separating — writing's planning time produces measurably richer lexis than speech, so each mode is practised for what it uniquely develops.

Communicative Language Teaching (CLT)

CLT holds that language is learned by using it to mean something, and that fluency and accuracy are complementary goals (Richards & Rodgers, 2014). Nothing in these four activities has a single correct answer. Students state opinions about their own habits, defend an assigned position on gamification, take a side on plastics policy, agree or disagree about social media. Language is the means; the message is the point.

Task-Based Learning (TBL)

In TBL the task drives the language, not the reverse (Willis & Willis, 2007). Each activity here ends in a real outcome: a posted argument video, a rebuttal, a critical question, a 200–250 word synthesis. Grammar and vocabulary appear because the task demands them — advanced conditionals surface in the debate because that is how you argue a counterfactual, not because a syllabus scheduled them for that week.

Student-Centred Learning

Control sits with the student. They choose which report to analyse, which position to take, which classmate to rebut, which of the two model syntheses to work from. The teacher sets the material and the constraints; the student supplies the content, the stance and the pace. The self-tracking workspaces and the asynchronous Padlet wall exist to make that autonomy practical — feedback arrives without waiting for the teacher.

05 — Creativity

What makes this sequence mine

Three deliberate choices go beyond selecting good material.

The material arrives in its native format

The TED Talk is not shown as a lecture recording — it is shown as a TikTok, in the feed, with the caption burned in and the translate button visible. The format is part of the authenticity, not a delivery inconvenience.

Two custom-built workspaces

Rather than a worksheet, both writing activities are web apps I coded and deployed — with browser speech synthesis for the quotes, self-tracking steps, instant True/False feedback, and a live lexical-diversity meter that turns a research finding into something a student can watch move while they type.

Assigned roles instead of free opinion

In the gamification debate, students argue the side they are given, not the side they hold. That single constraint converts a discussion into argumentation — and the timed video format on Padlet means every student, not just the confident ones, produces the same three minutes of English.
